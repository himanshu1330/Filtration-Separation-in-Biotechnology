Microfiltration
First-pass clarification & cell removal

Microfiltration serves as the primary clarification stage in biotechnological downstream processing. Membranes with pore sizes of 0.1–10 µm retain suspended particles, large colloids, and microbial cells while allowing dissolved macromolecules and salts to pass via convective flow through micropores.

  • Pore Size0.1 – 10 µm
  • Driving ForcePressure (0.1 – 2 bar)
  • MechanismSize Exclusion / Convection
  • MWCO> 300 kDa
  • TargetsBacteria, yeast, cell debris
  • Common MaterialsPVDF, PTFE, PES
Operating Pressure Intensity (relative to 80 bar max)

Key Applications

  • BioprocessingCell harvesting from fermentation broths
  • DairyDefatting milk, bacteria removal
  • BeveragesCold sterilization of wine & beer
  • WaterPretreatment for UF/RO systems

Advantages vs Centrifugation

  • ✅ Eliminates harsh chemicals for flocculation
  • ✅ Closed system — better containment
  • ✅ Superior recovery rates for microorganisms
  • ⚠️ Ineffective against viruses/small proteins
  • ⚠️ Requires subsequent finer filtration stages
🔬
Ultrafiltration
Molecular separation & concentration

Ultrafiltration bridges the gap between MF and RO. Operating at 1–100 nm pore sizes with MWCO of 1–1000 kDa, UF is ideal for concentrating and fractionating proteins, viruses, and macromolecules. Asymmetric membranes with a thin "skin" layer optimize flux and selectivity.

  • Pore Size1 – 100 nm
  • MWCO Range1 – 1000 kDa
  • Driving ForcePressure (1 – 5 bar)
  • MechanismSize Exclusion / Convection
  • TargetsProteins, viruses, macromolecules
  • Common MaterialsPES, PSf, regenerated cellulose

Diafiltration Integration

UF is frequently combined with diafiltration (DF), where fresh solvent is added to the retentate to "wash out" low-MW contaminants such as salts, sugars, and buffer components — achieving buffer exchange and desalting in a single unit operation.

Reverse Osmosis
Dense membrane, solution-diffusion mechanism

RO represents the highest degree of separation among pressure-driven processes. Using essentially non-porous membranes, it rejects virtually all dissolved salts and organics >100 g/mol. Water molecules dissolve into the dense polymer layer and diffuse across it driven by hydraulic pressure exceeding osmotic pressure.

  • Pore Size< 1 nm (dense, non-porous)
  • Driving ForcePressure (30 – 80 bar)
  • MechanismSolution-Diffusion
  • Salt Rejection> 99%
  • TargetsSalts, small organics, desalination
  • Common MaterialsAromatic polyamide TFC

Dialysis
Concentration-driven passive diffusion

Dialysis differs fundamentally from pressure-driven processes by using a concentration gradient as its driving force. Fine microporous membranes allow passage of small molecules based on size, conformation, and charge while retaining macromolecules. In hemodialysis, these membranes act as an artificial kidney.

  • Pore Size1 – 10 nm
  • Driving ForceConcentration gradient
  • MechanismPassive diffusion
  • TargetsIons, small metabolites, urea, creatinine
  • SpeedSlow (hours to days)
  • ScaleLab-scale / medical

Medical: Hemodialysis

Hemodialysis membranes function as an artificial kidney, removing uremic toxins (urea, creatinine) and excess fluid from blood. Hollow-fiber module geometry provides the high surface area required for adequate toxin clearance.

Bioprocessing Applications

From biopharmaceutical manufacturing to dairy valorization — membrane technology underpins the entire modern bioeconomy.

🧬 Biopharmaceuticals & mAbs

1. Clarification
Functionalized depth filters and quaternary amine-modified synthetic filters achieve clarification and HCP clearance in a single step — up to 17× better than conventional cellulose-based filters.
2. Membrane Chromatography
Membrane adsorbers in flow-through mode for polishing remove aggregates, HCPs, and DNA with higher volumetric throughput due to convective mass transfer.
3. Ultrafiltration / Diafiltration
Protein concentration and buffer exchange. SP-TFF enables continuous concentration without recirculation tanks, achieving "straight-through" purification.
4. Viral Clearance
Specialized UF membranes act as an absolute barrier for viral particles — a critical regulatory requirement for all therapeutic proteins.

🏭 Other Key Applications

🥛 Dairy Industry

UF isolates whey protein concentrates (WPC/WPI). UF-MILK is more efficient than UF-YOG for Greek yogurt production as post-fermentation UF causes severe membrane fouling from the acidified protein matrix.

🧫 Continuous Bioprocessing / VLP Vaccines

Integrated continuous downstream processes couple flow-through chromatography with UF and periodic counter-current chromatography, reducing facility footprint and batch-to-batch variation.

⚗️ Organic Acid Recovery

NF membranes (e.g., polyimide P84) demonstrate >90% rejection of succinate from fermentation broths. Electrodialysis selectively transports organic acid ions, with high recovery yields and low energy.

🌊 Water Treatment / MBR

Membrane Bioreactors (MBRs) show significantly lower global warming potential related to sludge removal compared to conventional activated sludge processes.

Fouling: Mechanisms & Mitigation

Membrane fouling remains the single most significant obstacle to long-term efficiency. Understanding fouling types is the first step to effective mitigation.

Types of Fouling

Accumulation of suspended solids and colloids on the membrane surface, typically forming a cake layer. The cake layer increases hydraulic resistance and reduces flux. Mitigation: backwashing, pre-filtration, coagulation/flocculation of feed.
Adsorption of dissolved organic matter (proteins, humic substances) driven by hydrophobic-hydrophobic interactions. BSA bridges with Ca²⁺ cations create dense fouled layers. Operating at pH far from protein's isoelectric point (IEP) where membrane and protein carry the same charge sign minimizes fouling.
Attachment and multiplication of microorganisms forming a resilient biofilm encased in extracellular polymeric substances (EPS). EPS includes polysaccharides, proteins, and nucleic acids that resist chemical cleaning. Biofouling is considered the most difficult fouling type to remove and can cause catastrophic flux decline.
Precipitation of salts (CaCO₃, BaSO₄, CaSO₄) when their concentration exceeds solubility at the membrane surface — particularly dangerous in RO due to high concentration polarization. Scaling reduces flux and can damage the membrane irreversibly. Antiscalants and pH adjustment are primary prevention strategies.

Mitigation Strategies

💨
Physical Methods

Backwashing, air scouring, and membrane relaxation dislodge cake layers without chemicals. Increasing crossflow velocity (CFV) generates shear forces that minimize concentration polarization at the membrane interface.

🧴
Chemical CIP

Regular Cleaning-in-Place using acids, bases, or enzymatic detergents removes adsorbed foulants. Ceramic membranes tolerate steam sterilization and bleach; polymeric membranes require milder protocols.

⚡
Zeta Potential Monitoring

High absolute zeta potential values indicate strong electrostatic repulsion. Monitoring changes in surface charge is a recognized early detection method for fouling and evaluates cleaning protocol effectiveness.

🤖
AI-Powered Predictive Modeling

Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) trained on historical data predict fouling rates and optimize cleaning frequencies. Digital Twins enable autonomous control for real-time fouling management.

Sustainability & Circular Economy

~14,000 tonnes of membranes are discarded globally each year. Innovative strategies are transforming end-of-life membranes into resources.

♻️
Downcycling
Retired RO membranes treated with sodium hypochlorite to remove the fouled polyamide layer, transforming them into recycled NF or UF membranes for lower-stringency applications like wastewater pretreatment.
⬆️
Upcycling
Converting retired MF or UF membranes into high-performance NF/RO membranes through surface functionalization and interfacial polymerization — adding value to end-of-life materials.
🔄
Covalent Adaptable Networks (CANs)
Advanced thermoset polymers with dynamic covalent bonds allow depolymerization and reprocessing at end-of-life — enabling true closed-loop recycling of membrane materials.
🌿
Green Solvents
Bio-based solvents Cyrene (dihydrolevoglucosenone) and lactic acid esters replace toxic NMP and DMF in membrane fabrication, reducing hazardous waste throughout the manufacturing process.
🔧
Mechanosynthesis
Solvent-free synthesis of membrane building blocks (MOFs, COFs) using mechanical energy (grinding, shearing) — drastically reducing chemical reagent use compared to conventional solvothermal methods.
🌾
Biopolymer Membranes
Chitosan, cellulose derivatives, and polyhydroxyalkanoates from renewable sources replace petroleum-derived polymers, reducing the environmental footprint of the membrane lifecycle from cradle to grave.
Future Directions

The convergence of biology, nanotechnology, and digital intelligence is defining the next generation of membrane separation systems.

01
🧬 Biomimetic Membranes & Aquaporins
Incorporating Aquaporin water channel proteins into synthetic polymer matrices could revolutionize desalination and resource recovery, achieving theoretical water fluxes far exceeding current TFC membranes while maintaining near-perfect ion rejection. These biological transport proteins selectively allow water passage while excluding ions and other solutes.
02
🤖 AI & Digital Twins
Digital Twins combined with Process Analytical Technology (PAT) enable real-time monitoring and autonomous control of membrane systems. Chemometric modeling and predictive analytics support "real-time release" (RTR) of products, maximizing cleaning efficiency and ensuring consistent quality in fully automated continuous biomanufacturing.
03
💎 Advanced Nanocomposites
MOF-integrated TFN membranes, graphene oxide barriers, and carbon nanotube-enhanced composites continue to push the boundaries of permeance and selectivity. These materials create engineered transport pathways at the sub-nanometer scale, enabling separations not achievable with conventional polymeric materials.

🏭 Hollow Fiber Manufacturing Technologies

🔥
Melt Spinning

Polymer heated above melting point and extruded through a spinneret, followed by immediate cooling. Produces dense, mechanically strong fibers without solvents.

💨
Dry Spinning

Polymer dissolved in volatile solvent; after extrusion, solvent evaporates using heat, causing solidification. Creates asymmetric pore structures.

💧
Wet Spinning

Polymer solution extruded into non-solvent bath where phase inversion occurs. Often involves an air gap to control membrane morphology and pore size.
